## How this packet is used

The recent Mark chats are lesson sources and same-chapter review references,
not refined app benchmarks or final chapter copy. Mark 6 alone sets the refined
app-quality bar. Selah Brain should author a fresh workup for each chapter after:

1. the v1.8 rule diff is independently reviewed and approved;
2. these chapter-only notes are owner-approved and added with `scope=chapter`;
3. the retrieval manifest proves that the intended rules, notes, example, and
   owner-approved source bundle are present;
4. the owner explicitly authorizes that chapter's generation run.

Private study-chat prose stays outside the authoring prompt and may be used only
for independent coverage or accuracy checks after generation. The exact
approved Mark 6 voice exemplar is the narrow exception: the writer may see it
to learn Selah's register, but must not reuse its distinctive lines, titles, or
phrasing. A separate freshness review remains required before owner acceptance.

The signed-in inventory audit confirmed recent Mark 8, Mark 9, and Mark 10
study chats. They may provide lessons and chapter-specific review references.
It did not find a distinct Mark 11 workup by title or passage search, so Mark 11
remains independently researched review guidance and must not be represented as
transcript-derived learning.

The generation reader currently retrieves at most ten chapter notes. Each list
below therefore stays within that real limit. A note is deliberately dense: it
defines a chapter-specific boundary without turning the whole lesson into a
global rule. Direction labels and memorable benchmark phrases are
comparison-only; they must not be loaded into the authoring prompt or copied as
titles, taglines, prayers, or application language.

Owner-selected prompt-time source for this New Testament sprint: the official
Crossway ESV API, **ESV Text Edition: 2025**. This is noncommercial ministry
analysis context, not model training. Crossway's published terms do not
explicitly address third-party model analysis; the owner accepts that
uncertainty without claiming a special Crossway license. See
`scripture-source-policy.md`.

To ground Book Flow without relying on model memory, the protected server
bundle must contain the primary ESV Mark chapter plus one ESV chapter on each
side. Each passage is role-labeled and digest-bound; a canonical bundle digest
also binds their order and fixed API options. ESV text must remain transient and
must not be committed, logged, persisted in a generated workup, or reproduced
in output.

## Mark 8 (`mark-8`)

Comparison direction (not an assigned title): **Learning to See Jesus
Clearly**.

1. Preserve every movement: compassion and the four thousand (1–10), the
   demanded sign (11–13), bread/leaven and failed memory (14–21), sight in two
   stages at Bethsaida (22–26), Peter's confession (27–30), Peter's resistance
   to the suffering path (31–33), and costly discipleship (34–38).
   Partial-to-clear sight may guide the chapter, but it must not flatten it.
2. The opening likely continues the broader Decapolis setting from Mark 7, but
   Mark 8 does not name the feeding site or say the crowd is entirely Gentile.
   Provision reveals Jesus' compassion; it does not promise every material
   shortage resolves on our timetable. Do not manufacture symbolic codes from
   the crowd, loaf, fish, or basket numbers.
3. The Pharisees' demand is a testing demand after abundant signs; do not imply
   that honest questions or careful evidence-seeking are sinful. Pharisees are
   Jewish interlocutors, not priests by definition, foreign outsiders, or
   visual villains.
4. The leaven of the Pharisees and Herod is a spreading influence, but its exact
   content is debated. Do not make Pharisees and Herodian supporters identical,
   and do not reduce the boat scene to the disciples being stupid: they are
   holding remembered evidence and still struggling to perceive what it means.
5. Preserve the staged healing exactly: Jesus leads the man outside Bethsaida,
   partial sight follows the first action, and clear sight follows the second.
   Mark does not explain why. Never describe Jesus as failing, experimenting,
   losing power, or being surprised; label its connection to the disciples'
   partial sight as strong literary inference.
6. Build Peter's confession from Mark alone. It occurs on the road among the
   villages around Caesarea Philippi, not explicitly at the Pan grotto, a pagan
   temple, a famous cliff, or Mount Hermon. Peter has the right title but resists
   Jesus' stated path; unstated expectations and motives remain interpretation.
7. Jesus' rebuke identifies Peter's counsel as adversarial to God's way; it does
   not say Peter is demon-possessed. Keep the sequence of suffering, rejection,
   death, and resurrection. Peter's acceptance of Jesus' identity alongside his
   resistance to Jesus' path is an interpretive contrast, not permission to
   invent his unstated expectations.
8. Mark 8:35 centers costly allegiance to Jesus and the good news, including
   real loss. A false self trying to be its own lord may be secondary
   application, not the verse's whole meaning. Cross-bearing never sanctifies
   abuse, coercion, burnout, self-harm, preventable danger, self-neglect, or
   suffering-seeking, and never tells someone to remain with an abuser.
9. Map only responsible regions: probable eastern/Decapolis context,
   unidentified Dalmanutha, debated Bethsaida, and the villages around Caesarea
   Philippi. Use uncertainty regions rather than false pins or a verified route.
   Images remain people-first and must not merge separate moments or import
   parallel-Gospel details.
10. Image candidates should distinguish the chapter's movements rather than
   repeat a feeding tableau: the seven baskets after the crowd has eaten can
   carry the evidence-and-memory theme; a restrained subjective partial-sight
   treatment may illuminate the staged healing; and a Peter-rebuke scene should
   retain the other disciples because Jesus turns and sees them. Never place
   the remembered baskets in the boat, depict Satan as a visible figure, or
   portray Peter as possessed. These are candidates, not mandatory image slots;
   Selah still chooses the strongest chapter-shaped set.

## Mark 9 (`mark-9`)

Comparison direction (not an assigned title): **Glory and Failure, Side by
Side**. Controlling emphasis: listening to Jesus.

1. Preserve the full movement: the hinge saying (1), Transfiguration (2–8),
   descent and Elijah (9–13), suffering boy and father (14–29), passion prediction
   (30–32), greatness and the child (33–37), the person outside the Twelve's
   circle (38–41), and stumbling, radical holiness, salt, and peace (42–50).
   Explain how the mountain scene and the following failed-ministry scene
   interpret one another without collapsing either.
2. The Transfiguration's placement makes it a strong interpretive foretaste of
   9:1, but whether it exhausts the saying is debated. Mark does not name the
   mountain. Tabor is traditional; a northern/Hermon-region proposal follows
   the preceding geography. Moses-and-Elijah meanings, Peter's shelter
   symbolism, and exact visual extent are interpretation; show no humanoid
   Father, halo, glowing
   eyes, beams, or identifiable mountain landmark.
3. Mark describes symptoms that are seizure-like to a modern reader and
   explicitly identifies a spirit in this particular story. Do not diagnose
   epilepsy retrospectively, generalize seizures, disability, or mental illness as
   demonic, blame the boy or father, displace medical care, or use convulsion as
   visual spectacle. Compassion and restored dignity come before explanation.
4. The father's mixed faith is brought honestly to Jesus and met without shame.
   Treat faith as potentially genuine even when emotionally quiet,
   divided, or plainly expressed; write this insight freshly rather than
   reproducing a study-chat line. Do not promise this episode's timing or outcome
   to every suffering family. Continued suffering is never proof of deficient
   faith.
5. Prayer in 9:29 means dependence on God, not a technique, power ranking,
   correct phrase, or guaranteed method. Some manuscripts add fasting; treat it
   as an edition-specific textual note. Do not invent a taxonomy behind “this
   kind.”
6. Hold glory and the passion prediction together: Jesus' glory is revealed in
   willing, cross-shaped love. Suffering is not inherently holy, and this text
   never licenses abuse, burnout, self-neglect, avoidable harm, or seeking pain.
7. In Capernaum, Jesus answers a status argument with a child. Children could be
   loved while lacking adult rank and power; do not call them worthless. The
   child's age, gender, household, and identity are unknown. Prefer a
   child standing beside or within Jesus' arm rather than a default lap pose.
8. The unnamed person acting in Jesus' name corrects possessive gatekeeping, not
   all discernment. Do not call him an unbeliever, heretic, rival leader, or
   secret apostle. The disciples do not own Jesus' work or get to equate their
   circle with the whole kingdom.
9. Hand, foot, and eye removal is severe rhetorical hyperbole demanding decisive
   resistance to sin, never bodily injury. Apply it to access, secrecy, habits,
   environments, lies, or systems. Gehenna should not be called a perpetually
   burning first-century city dump. Salt-and-fire wording and meaning are
   difficult and must remain transparent; the clear landing is faithful
   distinctiveness and peace.
10. Keep separate scenes separate: the suffering boy, the child in the greatness
    lesson, and the millstone warning are not one child or one staged moment.
    Map Galilee and Capernaum with an unnamed mountain and unknown route. Prefer the
    restored-by-the-hand human moment as hero over medical spectacle or
    superhero Transfiguration imagery.

## Mark 10 (`mark-10`)

Comparison direction (not an assigned title): **Open Hands on the Way**.

1. Preserve the full road-to-Jerusalem movement: divorce and hard hearts (1–12),
   children received (13–16), the wealthy man and divine possibility (17–31),
   passion prediction (32–34), James and John and servant greatness (35–45), and
   Bartimaeus receiving mercy and following on the way (46–52). Track how the
   scenes test attempts to secure kingdom life through legal control, status,
   possessions, rank, or crowd power, while Jesus centers receiving, service,
   and following.
2. Jesus returns the divorce test from legal permission to God's joining intent
   and human hardness. Mark 10 is not a complete modern pastoral policy and does
   not explicitly address abuse. Never use it to trap someone in danger,
   minimize harm, force unsafe reunion, or make a generated draft decide complex
   pastoral care.
3. Children are textually welcomed and blessed; Mark does not define them as
   morally innocent, identify all caregivers as mothers, or give exact ages.
   Dependence, receptivity, and low social rank are responsible interpretations,
   not direct definitions.
4. In Mark-local copy call him **the wealthy man**. Matthew supplies “young” and
   Luke supplies “ruler.” Do not import a youthful face, official title, signet,
   guards, colors, or authority symbols. Jesus' look and love, the man's fallen
   response, grief, many possessions, and departure are explicit; his sincerity,
   greed, inner monologue, later fate, and permanent rejection are unknown.
5. Do not weaken the wealth warning into a claim that money is neutral whenever
   one's attitude is good, and do not invent the later Needle Gate legend. The direct command is
   to this man; Mark's broad warning remains intentionally unsettling. Human
   salvation is impossible as an achievement and possible with God; do not turn
   the scene into a prosperity or poverty formula.
6. Jesus walks ahead and names rejection, death, and resurrection. His forward
   position and repeated prediction support a narrative/theological synthesis
   of willing movement toward suffering, but not a camera-level claim about
   every private thought. The disciples' amazement and fear are explicit; exact
   route and expressions remain reconstruction.
7. James and John ask for honor immediately after the passion prediction. Do
   not make them cartoonishly selfish or assume the other ten's indignation is
   purer. Kingdom greatness takes the shape of service because Jesus' mission
   takes the shape of costly self-giving; “ransom for many” must not be forced
   to carry every detail of one atonement theory.
8. Bartimaeus is named, calls for mercy, receives sight, and follows on the way.
   Mark creates a strong contrast with status-seeking, but does not say he fully
   understands every part of Jesus' mission. Portray disability with dignity:
   no fabricated diagnosis, white or clouded eyes, helpless spectacle, or claim
   that his cloak was his only possession or proves total surrender.
9. Historic map: broad Judea and trans-Jordan context, Jordan Valley, Jericho region,
   and Jerusalem as destination; dashed possible movement only. Do not pin
   teaching scenes or the healing roadside. Modern borders must not be projected
   backward into first-century regions.
10. Images remain people-first: the wealthy man's observable grief under
    Jesus' loving attention; varied children and caregivers; Jesus walking ahead;
    servant-greatness correction without thrones or foot washing; Bartimaeus
    called into the center. No divorce tableau, Needle Gate, synoptic identity
    imports, or invented interior motives.

## Mark 11 (`mark-11`)

Comparison direction (not an assigned title): **The King Who Looks for
Fruit**. Alternate comparison direction: **When Jesus Looks Around**.

1. Preserve Mark's three-day narrative sequence: entry, late Temple inspection,
   and return to Bethany (1–11); next-day fig tree (12–14) and Temple action
   (15–19); following-morning withered tree, prayer and forgiveness teaching
   (20–26), and authority challenge (27–33). Do not label Sunday, Monday, or
   Tuesday as text-explicit.
2. Mark names one unridden young animal, cloaks, and leafy material cut from
   fields. A donkey is highly probable from Zechariah and parallel accounts, but
   Mark's noun is broader. Mark does not identify palms, waving, the two
   disciples, prior arrangement, the entry gate, crowd size, or the weekday.
   Do not say this is the same crowd later involved in Jesus' death.
3. Jesus first looks around the Temple and leaves because it is late; the next
   day's action is narratively measured, not an arrival tantrum. The fig tree is
   leafy, fruitless, and explicitly out of season. Mark's interwoven tree and
   Temple structure strongly invites interpretation, but a reading of fruitless
   worship or Temple order under judgment must remain labeled and must never become a curse on
   Jewish people or ethnic Israel. Do not solve the season tension with folklore
   or depict an instant special effect.
4. Mark places Jesus in the broad Temple precinct. The Court of the Gentiles is
   a plausible reconstruction, not explicit. Exchange and animal sales served
   legitimate pilgrimage and sacrifice needs; Mark alleges no dishonest rate. Use
   “Temple action” before explaining judgment or cleansing. No whip, injuries,
   rage claim, violent assault, inner sanctuary, or movie brawl.
5. Read Jesus' quotations in their prophetic contexts: Isaiah 56 centers prayer
   and inclusion of peoples otherwise excluded; Jeremiah 7 condemns injustice,
   violence, idolatry, and using sacred space as refuge from accountability.
   The den-of-robbers image is larger than retail price-gouging. Jesus, disciples,
   leaders, merchants, and crowd are Jewish; name the particular groups Mark
   names and reject anti-Jewish caricature or replacement theology.
6. The withered tree is discovered the following morning; Peter notices it.
   Jesus directs faith toward God, not confidence in confidence. The mountain
   saying may be rhetorical impossibility, Mount of Olives and Temple context, or
   another referent; do not settle it. Never promise health, wealth, or any
   request on demand, or blame unanswered prayer and suffering on weak faith.
7. Forgiveness does not mean calling harm good, waiving accountability,
   restoring trust or access automatically, reconciling without repentance, or
   remaining in danger. A prayer can release vengeance to God while keeping
   truth, justice, boundaries, and safety.
8. Mark 11:26 is a genuine textual and numbering variant: important earlier
   witnesses omit the additional sentence while later manuscripts include a
   parallel to Matthew. Follow the licensed reader edition and explain calmly;
   verse 25 already contains Mark's forgiveness teaching.
9. The authority challenge names chief priests, teachers of the Law, and elders.
   Their recorded deliberation makes the answer strategic, but do not invent
   additional private motives, name Caiaphas, call it a formal Sanhedrin session,
   add Pharisees, Sadducees, or Roman soldiers, or make leaders racially distinct
   villains. A theological synthesis may locate Jesus' authority in Mark's
   broader presentation of God's Son, but must be labeled rather than presented
   as a camera-level fact of this exchange.
10. Map Bethany, the Mount of Olives ridge, Jerusalem and the Temple, and approximate
    Bethphage or a representative approach; no exact road, gate, fig tree, or vendor
    court. Images: humble entry without palm-pageant harmonization; quiet Temple
    inspection; restrained Temple action plus teaching; withered-tree discovery;
    controlled authority confrontation. Use the Herodian and Second Temple world,
    with no modern Dome, Solomon's Temple, pseudo-readable sacred text, whip, gore,
    caricature, or identical mob.

## Comparison rubric after generation

The generated draft is compared with the corrected workup benchmark, not for
word-for-word similarity but for:

1. complete coverage of the chapter's movements;
2. Mark-local facts and labeled synoptic context;
3. text / inference / interpretation / unknown discipline;
4. Selah's wise-friend cadence and memorable, natural lines;
5. Jesus-centered theological depth without forced connections;
6. historical, geographic, medical, disability, and pastoral integrity;
7. chapter-shaped prayer and application without promises, blame, or harm;
8. image/map/completion readiness;
9. no licensed Bible text in generated output;
10. no copying of private-source wording merely because it was a benchmark.

Operational comparison uses the versioned 100-point rubric in
`lib/ai/quality/selah-benchmark-rubric.v2.json`. It expands these ten concerns
into thirteen independently evidenced dimensions. The provisional gate
requires at least 85/100 with no dimension below the publishable floor and
requires benchmark-level Selah voice, source integrity, and fresh authorship.
A machine may validate the review artifact and calculate the score; it may not
declare the chapter owner-approved. Weak dimensions receive typed, targeted
remediation rather than a blind full-chapter regeneration.

Systemic misses become reviewed rule amendments. Chapter-only misses become
chapter notes. Mechanical misses become code QA. A weak section receives a
targeted revision; Selah does not blindly regenerate the whole approved draft.
