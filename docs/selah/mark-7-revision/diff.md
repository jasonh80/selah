### `insights[chapter-flow].body`

**Live (base):**
> In verses 1–5, Pharisees and scribes from Jerusalem question Jesus because some disciples do not follow the traditional washing custom before eating. Mark explains the practice because many of his readers likely need the background. This is about ritual purity and tradition, not whether the disciples owned soap.
> 
> In verses 6–13, Jesus answers by bringing Scripture to bear. He says the leaders are in danger of replacing God's command with human tradition. The example is Corban: a resource declared dedicated to God could be used to avoid helping one's parents. Jesus' point is sharp because it is concrete. The test of our theology is often not whether it sounds impressive, but whether it produces obedience in the room where love costs us something.
> 
> In verses 14–23, Jesus calls the crowd and then explains privately to His disciples that defilement comes from the heart. Mark's note about foods being clean is brief, but enormous. The kingdom Jesus brings will not be contained by the old boundary markers in the same way, because the deeper uncleanness is not in the meal but in the human will.
> 
> In verses 24–30, Jesus goes to the region of Tyre and Sidon. A Gentile mother pleads for her daughter. The exchange about children, bread, and dogs is difficult, but her reply is remarkable: she does not demand the children's place; she trusts that even the overflow of Jesus' mercy is enough. Jesus grants her request.
> 
> In verses 31–37, Jesus travels by an unusual route toward the Decapolis. People bring Him a deaf man with a speech difficulty. Jesus takes him aside privately, uses touch, looks heavenward, sighs, and speaks an Aramaic command meaning to be opened. The man is restored, and the crowd's amazement spills over despite Jesus' warning to keep quiet.

**Proposed:**
> Verses 1–5 open with a dispute about ritual washing—not soap, germs, or lazy disciples. Pharisees and scribes ask why Jesus’ disciples do not follow the inherited practice.
> 
> In verses 6–13, Jesus brings the conversation out of theory. A Corban vow could sound devoted while helping someone avoid caring for aging parents. Religious language can hide ordinary disobedience. Apparently humans can turn almost anything into a loophole.
> 
> In verses 14–23, Jesus calls the crowd closer and moves the problem from the meal to the heart. Food is not the deepest threat. The greed, deceit, pride, cruelty, and foolishness coming from within us are.
> 
> In verses 24–30, a Gentile mother asks Jesus to free her daughter. The exchange is hard, and Mark does not explain every motive. What he does show is her bold trust and Jesus’ mercy reaching her home.
> 
> In verses 31–37, people bring Jesus a deaf man with a speech difficulty. Jesus takes him away from the crowd, touches him, sighs, and says, “Ephphatha”—“Be opened.” The chapter that exposed hidden hearts ends with personal dignity and restoration.

### `insights[jesus].body`

**Live (base):**
> Jesus stands at the center of Mark 7 with an authority no mere teacher can safely claim on his own. He judges tradition by God's command. He interprets what truly defiles a person. He speaks in a way that Mark understands to reshape how foods and purity boundaries are seen in the kingdom of God.
> 
> But His holiness is not fragile. That may be one of the most beautiful things in the chapter. Jesus enters a house in Gentile territory. A mother with an afflicted daughter falls at His feet. A deaf man is brought to Him in the Decapolis. None of this threatens Him. He does not need distance to remain clean. His purity moves outward as mercy.
> 
> That is the gospel shape. Jesus does not look at the unclean and step back in fear. He comes near, tells the truth, bears our sin, and makes people whole. Mark will later show Him going to the cross, where human evil comes out in full force and Jesus answers it with obedient love. The One who opens ears in chapter 7 will soon open the way to God through His death and resurrection.

**Proposed:**
> Jesus does not merely join the purity debate. He tells the truth about what makes us unclean: the trouble is not only out there; it lives in the human heart. That is a claim no ordinary teacher can carry lightly.
> 
> And yet His holiness is not fragile. He enters Gentile territory, receives a desperate mother, and takes a deaf man away from the crowd before healing him. Jesus exposes what is wrong without treating hurting people like examples in a lecture. He comes close.
> 
> That is the gospel shape of the chapter. The One who names our uncleanness will carry our sin to the cross. He tells the truth because He came to save, not to score points. The heart He exposes is the heart He is willing to remake.

### `insights[theology].body`

**Live (base):**
> The theology word here is defilement. It means uncleanness before God. In the Old Testament, purity laws taught Israel that God is holy and that life with Him cannot be treated casually. Those laws formed a people who learned to distinguish clean from unclean, holy from common.
> 
> In Mark 7, Jesus does not say holiness no longer matters. He says the deepest source of uncleanness is within us. Food passes through the body; evil comes from the heart. That is a devastatingly simple diagnosis. It means we cannot solve sin only by controlling our environment, curating our image, or keeping the right distance from the wrong people.
> 
> This matters for life with Jesus because He does not merely polish behavior. He cleanses, forgives, teaches, and remakes. The Christian life is not pretending the heart is fine while arranging religious objects around it. It is bringing the heart into the light of Christ and letting Him rule there.

**Proposed:**
> The Bible’s word here is defilement—being unclean before God. Israel’s purity laws taught that God is holy and life with Him matters. Jesus does not toss holiness aside. He takes the question deeper.
> 
> Food passes through the body. Greed, deceit, cruelty, pride, and the rest come from within. We humans would prefer a problem we could fix with better habits and a respectable schedule. Jesus refuses to flatter us: the heart needs more than polish.
> 
> That is why grace is good news. Jesus does not only tell us to try harder. He forgives, cleanses, teaches, and remakes. We can stop decorating the hiding place and bring the real heart into His light.

### `insights[application].body`

**Live (base):**
> Mark 7 gives us a brave and uncomfortable place to pray from: Lord, show me where I use good things badly. A habit can be good. A tradition can be good. A boundary can be wise. But if any of them help us avoid love, repentance, truth, or obedience, they have become a hiding place.
> 
> So the application is not, 'Throw away every tradition.' It is, 'Bring every tradition under the word of God.' Does this practice help me love God and neighbor? Does it train humility? Does it make me quicker to obey, or just quicker to judge?
> 
> And when Jesus exposes the heart, do not run. The same chapter that names inner evil also shows Jesus answering a desperate mother and tenderly restoring a man who could not hear or speak plainly. His truth is not a weapon to keep the needy away. It is the surgeon's light before healing.
> 
> A simple practice: read Jesus' list of heart-sins slowly. Pause where one stings. Do not negotiate. Do not spiral. Bring it to Him plainly. The heart Jesus exposes is the heart He came to save.

**Proposed:**
> Before Mark 7 challenges you, notice where it ends: Jesus answering a desperate mother and treating a deaf man with quiet dignity. The One who exposes the heart is not looking for a reason to crush you. He is making room for honesty and healing.
> 
> Now ask the uncomfortable question: Where am I using a good habit, tradition, or boundary to avoid plain obedience? Does it help me love God and people, or mostly help me look right?
> 
> Read Jesus’ list of heart-sins slowly. Pause where one stings. Do not negotiate with it. Do not spiral either. Name it honestly, bring it to Jesus, and take the next faithful step. His truth is not a trap. The heart He exposes is the heart He came to save.
